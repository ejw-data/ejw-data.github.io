Projects

I must say that I think in terms of project management. I split most types of work I do into categories and each one I manage independently. I have enjoyed this philosophy for many years since I can always have multiple projects occurring at any given time.

I may start a project in January with full knowledge that I will not do very much on it until June but the resource assessment, the milestones, the key performance indicators, bottleneck troubleshooting, and time restraints are part of the planning from the very beginning.

Recently, I have been reading about Agile methodologies including Kanban and Scrum and comparing these methods to my experiences with Six Sigma and other processes like Stage Gate methods.

Below are some of my projects that I can release information about. Clicking the image will cause it to enlarge. Better quality images will replace the current images. Originally, the enlarging feature was not planned.

  • Business & Administration

  • Email Reduction to Increase Read Frequency

    Too many emails; no one opened them; mass confusion ensued. That was the problem. In response, I created an announcement email that I sent weekly providing updates about upcoming events with links and forms for additional information and to sign-up.

    Other changes were also implemented. For example, a common request was for volunteers, but students were hesitant to join without knowing the time commitment involved. In response, all sign-up forms began including an agenda with expectations and the contact information of the project leader. This simple change greatly impacted the responses.

    Another common request was for students to change bad behaviors - the "don't do this anymore" request. As it turned out, most students did not have the prior knowledge to know what they should do. So templates, checklists, and other simple notifications were used to bridge the gap and improve conditions.

    All these responses were integrated into the weekly emails.

  • Education Program

    Over 12,000 participants of education programs.

The largest contributers were pre-college education outreach (6,700 attendees), student seminars and professional development (4,700 attendees), and university recruitment (600 attendees).     One of the busiest parts of the year was between mid-October and mid-November of each year. This was the time when preparations for an NSF research center review (for continued funding) was in full swing. This event brought in many of the members of the research center - 50+ industry members, 20+ faculty, 120+ graduate student researchers, 50+ undergraduate researchers, 4 deans, and members of the scientific advisory board.

    Typically organized like a conference and run by students, the two day event had seminars, lab tours, poster sessions, guest speakers, and the ever important meals (ask any student). My role was unofficial. I did not book the hotel meeting rooms nor arrange the food. I did not collect abstracts or posters or print materials. I provided advice and reviews of the work each committee was performing - I provided real-time feedback from an organizational perspective.

  • Undergraduate Researcher Recruitment

    Over 150 research positions added over four years compared to 17 positions in the previous year.

    The ultimate goal was to have a 2:1 ratio of graduate students to undergraduate students. Each year a variety of methods were employed to increase the number of undergraduate students interested in studying power and energy. Events organized included open houses (lab tours, faculty discussions, etc.) highlighting research, speaking at junior seminars, advertising at semester welcome back events, holding special seminars for freshman diverse students, and using current student researchers contacts. In 2017, over 40 undergraduates were performing power and energy research.

  • Undergraduate Matriculation to Graduate School

    About 50% of CURENT undergrad researchers entered into graduate school

    To better prepare students for graduate school, the students performed research, presented at poster sessions, helped with education outreach, attended professional development seminars, participated in internships, and were sometimes sponsored to perform international research. Students too early in their coursework were also incorporated in the group by working in the office area as my student workers. All the students as a group were called the CURENT Ambassadors.

    Undergraduate enrollment into the masters power program was very low but upon creating the Ambassador program, establishing a 5-year BS/MS program, and two years of developing the undergraduate research program, the first two researchers started the program with multiple students starting each semester afterwards. With two more years, over 26 undergraduates had entered graduate school and 23 undergraduates had gone into industry with over a third going into power and energy fields.

  • REU and Undergrad Summer Research Recruitment

    About 41% of CURENT REU's entered into graduate school and the number of highly qualified applicants increased and was more diverse.

    After seeking out students through conferences, website advertising, social media, and email campaigns, the Research Experience for Undergraduates (REU) program had decent success. Students were from across the country performed research for eight weeks during the summer. Students attend professional development seminars, technical seminars, field trips, research meetings, and a poster symposium.

    With more than half of the past REU students still in college, the most recent graduates had 41% going to graduate school, 41% going to industry, and 18% with unknown status. The summer undergraduate program had similar results. The first two cohorts had under 15% of students attending graduate school, but since changing processes cohort 3 and cohort 4 had about 50% of the students going to grad school. The past two cohorts (5,6) are unknown their success since most of the students are still working on their BS.

    The application process also showed improvements. The first few year's applications had about 24 highly qualified candidates annually but those candidates were only 13% female and less than 29% under-represented minorities. In 2017, there were 43 highly qualified applicants (79% increase) with 37% of candidates being female (~3x increase) and 37% of candidates being under-represented minorities.

  • eVol9 Convention

    About 100 students participated in the AC power grid project.

    For several years I had been interested in developing a hands-on project using low voltage AC electricity. I had made several learning modules using DC electricity but they were always very simple since power conversion is very limited with simple equipment. An AC electricity project with transformers could represent many different real-life events but there are issues surrounding a cost effective power source and safety. All of this was resolved when I found 2.5v AC adapters for $8 and 10:1 turns power transformers for $12. With student workers supervising each table, I was able to implement safely a power grid project where 9th grade students supplied their system with 2.5v AC and transmitted the power at 24v AC and reduced back to a usable 2.5v AC to power lights. The students also used relays representing substation switches in their development of a mini-grid.

    This project was part of a 2.5 hour activity period that was held four times over the course of two days. About 100 students worked on the power grid project. I also organized a panel discussion for parents to interview undergraduate engineering students about their college experiences and I presented a seminar about how to develop collaborative relationships with universities and industry to increase real world connections.

  • Science and Engineering Festival Booth Design

    Over 2,500 people visited the booth. The activities are still being used and have been used by over 6,000 kids.

    In early 2014, I was asked to design a booth with interactive activities for the 2014 Science and Engineering Festival held in Washington DC. The festival was expected to have over 250,000 visitors and ended up having over 325,000 visitors. So with less than three months to spare and a budget less than $8,000, I needed to decide how I could provide a learning module that is entertaining, STEM related, takes less than 3 minutes to complete, and required few consumable materials. On top of these requirements, the event occurred 14 days before the NSF/DOE

    I decided that I would modify two activities students had enjoyed previously - solar car racing and circuits kits. I would also use a plasma ball but unaltered.

    The original solar car activity had students with lamps following the cars on the ground. It was an activity for small children which made it even more chaotic and not a good activity for a convention center. So to contain the activity, a frame was created that could sit on a standard 6' table with two lamps hanging from the upper frame. Additional controls were also added with a push button power cord and lamps without off options. This addition allowed for the lamps to be turned on and off simultaneously.

    The circuit kits have many loose parts and require a lot of explanation by a mentor. To make the kit durable, the kit components - light bulbs, switches, pushbuttons, variable resistors - were mounted on plastic boards that are freestanding on tables. The only not attached parts were the wires used to make the circuits. Powered with a 12 volt DC or less power adapters, a total of three boards were made each teaching a different concept about circuits.

    The activities worked well for the three day event and we saw over 2,500 people. I found a graduate student in electrical engineering that was available and four intern teachers who I trained to be able to explain and officiate each activity. After negotiating hotel rates and scheduling flights for half the helpers and driving the materials to DC in a van, we finished the event $100 below budget. Not bad for a 3 day stay in Washington, DC. Note: The activities had been used at other events totaling close to 6,000 students and only had small repairs needed.
